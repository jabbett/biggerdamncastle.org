Brandeis University's oldest, most iconic building meets the wrecking ball

Interim President Lisa Lynch announced on 25 January 2016 that most of Usen Castle—Towers C, D, E, and Schwartz—will be destroyed to construct new student housing.

We, the undersigned, feel that the only acceptable reason to destroy part of the Castle is to build a bigger damn castle.

We expect the University to maintain the Castle quad's whimsical, medieval character in any new construction, consistent with its 1979 placement on the National Register of Historic Places.

Historical significance of Usen Castle

Massachusetts Historical Commission, 1977

"The Usen Castle is a most unusual and historically significant building. It was built by Dr. John Hall Smith, founder of Middlesex Medical School, in 1928. In 1945 the school had been forced out of existence by the simple fact that few of its graduates were granted licenses to practice. Facing the immediate prospect of closing, school officials decided to pass its property and charter on to a group who in 1948 founded Brandeis University. The structure is one of two buildings on the Brandeis campus surviving from the former college. Employing local labor, and using second-hand windows and mechanical fixtures, bits of mosaic and painted decoration, Dr. Smith constructed a building inspired by a sketch of Ireland's Cavendish Castle. The fieldstone that was used for the walls had divided the site of the campus into narrow strips where local farmers grazed their cattle in colonial times. Dr. Smith built his Castle atop Boston Rock, one of the highest elevation points on the campus, and the entire Boston area as well. It was from this point that Colonel William Bradford first surveyed Boston in the mid-17th century."

"In the early years of Brandeis University, the Castle housed administrative and dining services as well as student dormitory space. In the 1950s, its 50,000 square feet were remodeled into ninety single, double and larger living units to house 142 students. Although the Castle had been deteriorating inside and out in recent years, it remained the overwhelming choice of the 2,100 students who reside on campus each year. Probably chief among the Castle's many attractions is its medieval air, an atmosphere enhanced by stone archways, sharply defined battlements, turrets, conical towers and spiral staircases. On this modern campus, it represents stone surrounded by glass, age by youth, tradition by innovation and change. The Castle's recent restoration assures the preservation of a symbolic historical structure and the building's continued use as a student residence for many decades to come."

—Joel E. Grushkin, Student of Architectural History and Director of Waltham Historic Survey Procedures
